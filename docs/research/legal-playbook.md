## 1. CRITICAL LEGAL PRINCIPLE: Who Owns the Minerals? {#1-critical-legal-principle}

### The Law Is Clear: The Family Does NOT Own the Minerals

**Mining Act, 2016, Section 7 — Ownership of Minerals:**

> "(1) Every mineral — (a) in its natural state in, under or upon any land in Kenya; (b) in or under any water; or (c) comprised in any tailings or mine waste, is **vested in the national government** to be held in trust for the people of Kenya."

**Constitution of Kenya, 2010, Article 62(1)(f):** Minerals and mineral oils are classified as **public land**, held by the national government in trust.

### What This Means for the Family

- **The minerals beneath their land belong to the Republic of Kenya**, not the family.
- The family **cannot sell minerals** directly — only the national government can grant mineral rights.
- **However**, the family has critical rights as landowners that no miner (Chinese or otherwise) can override:
  - Right to **consent** before mining operations begin on their land
  - Right to **fair compensation** for land use and disturbance
  - Right to **participate** in mining benefits (Community Development Agreement)
  - Right to **refuse entry** until proper procedures are followed
  - Right to **prior consultation** under the Fair Administrative Action Act, 2015

### Key Distinction: Land Rights vs. Mineral Rights

| Right | Who Holds It | Legal Basis |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| Ownership of land surface | The family (if titled) | Constitution Art. 40; Land Act, 2012 |
| Ownership of minerals | National Government | Mining Act s.7; Constitution Art. 62(1)(f) |
| Right to mine | Licensed miner only | Mining Act ss. 57-106 |
| Right to compensation | Landowner | Mining Act s. 172; Land Act s. 127 |
| Right to refuse entry | Landowner (until compensated) | Mining Act s. 172(2) |

## 2. RIGHTS OF THE FAMILY AS LANDOWNERS {#2-landowner-rights}

### A. Consent and Consultation Rights

**Mining Act, Section 172 — Compensation for disturbance of rights:**

Before any mining licence holder can enter or use private land, they **must**:

1. **Serve written notice** on the landowner (s. 172(1))
2. **Negotiate compensation** for:
   - Loss of use of surface land
   - Damage to crops, trees, or buildings
   - Severance (loss of value to remaining land)
   - Disturbance and inconvenience
3. **Pay compensation** or deposit it with the National Land Commission before entry

**If the Chinese miners have NOT done this, they are operating illegally.**

### B. Community Development Agreement (CDA)

**Mining Act, Section 173-174:** Holders of **large-scale mining licences** must enter into a Community Development Agreement with affected communities. This CDA must include:

- Employment and training opportunities for locals
- Procurement of local goods and services
- Community infrastructure development
- Environmental protection measures
- Revenue sharing arrangements

### C. Environmental Rights

**Constitution, Article  42:** Every person has the right to a clean and healthy environment.

**Environmental Management and Coordination Act (EMCA), 1999:** Any mining operation **must** obtain an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) licence before commencing. The family can:

- Demand to see the EIA licence
- Object to the EIA during public consultation
- Report violations to NEMA (National Environment Management Authority)

### D. Right to Fair Administrative Action

**Constitution, Article 47; Fair Administrative Action Act, 2015:** The family has the right to:

- Be given written reasons for any administrative decision affecting them
- Be heard before any decision is made
- Challenge any administrative action in court

## 3. ANTI-EXPLOITATION LEGAL TOOLS {#3-anti-exploitation}

### A. What Makes a Mineral Contract VOIDable

Under the **Law of Contract Act (Cap. 23)** and general principles of equity, a contract is voidable if:

1. **Undue influence** (s. 16-19): If the Chinese miners used their superior position, knowledge, or resources to pressure the family
2. **Misrepresentation** (s. 17-18): If they misrepresented the value of minerals, the terms, or their authority
3. **Fraud** (s. 17): If they deliberately concealed material facts (e.g., true mineral value)
4. **Unconscionability**: If the terms are so grossly unfair that no reasonable person would agree
5. **Lack of capacity**: If the family didn't understand what they were signing (language barrier, no legal advice)
6. **Illegality**: If the Chinese miners don't have proper mining licences

### B. Red Flags of Exploitation — Document Everything

| Red Flag | What to Record |
|----------|---------------|
| Miners testing minerals without family's knowledge | Photos, dates, witnesses |
| Cash payments with no receipts | Refuse cash; demand bank transfers |
| Contracts in Chinese only | Demand certified Swahili/English translation |
| No copy of contract given to family | This alone may void the contract |
| Threats or intimidation | Report to police immediately |
| Mining without licence visible on site | Report to County Mines Officer |
| Undervaluation of minerals | Get independent assay (see Section 4) |

### C. How to Demand Transparency in Mineral Testing

**Mining Act, Section 152:** The holder of a mineral right must keep accurate records of all minerals won and their disposal.

**The family can demand:**

1. **Written assay results** from an accredited laboratory
2. **Independent sampling** — the family has the right to take their own samples and send them to a different lab
3. **Witnessing of all testing** — the family or their representative can be present during mineral testing
4. **Full disclosure** of the mineral type, grade, and estimated value

**Recommended independent laboratories in Kenya:**
- **Government Chemist** (Nairobi) — free/low cost
- **Bureau Veritas Kenya** (Nairobi)
- **SGS Kenya** (Nairobi, Mombasa)
- **Intertek Kenya** (Nairobi)

### D. How to Challenge Unfair Valuation

If minerals are being bought at below market value:

1. **Get independent assay** from a lab listed above
2. **Compare with London Metal Exchange (LME)** or **Kitco** spot prices
3. **Document the difference** — if Chinese miners are paying less than 50% of market value, this is likely fraudulent
4. **File complaint** with:
   - County Mines and Geology Officer (Migori)
   - Directorate of Mines, Ministry of Mining
   - Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission (EACC) if officials are complicit

## 4. HOW TO GET AN INDEPENDENT MINERAL ASSESSMENT {#4-independent-assessment}

### Step-by-Step Process

**1. Collect Samples Properly**
- Take samples from multiple locations on the property
- Use clean containers (not previously used for chemicals)
- Record GPS coordinates, depth, and date for each sample
- Have a witness present (preferably a community elder or lawyer)

**2. Send to Accredited Laboratory**
- **Government Chemist Department**, Nairobi — Tel: +254 20 318081
  - Cost: ~KES 2,000–5,000 per sample (~$15–40 USD)
  - Turnaround: 7-14 days
- **SGS Kenya**, Nairobi — Tel: +254 20 6939000
  - Cost: ~KES 10,000–25,000 per sample (~$75–190 USD)
  - Turnaround: 3-7 days

**3. Request a Geological Survey**
- Contact the **Directorate of Geological Survey** (under the Ministry of Mining)
- They can conduct a survey of the family's land
- This establishes what minerals are present and their estimated quantities
- Cost: Free for basic assessment (government service)

**4. Hire an Independent Geologist**
- Must be registered under the **Geologists Registration Act (Cap. 535)**
- Can be found through the **Geological Society of Kenya**
- Cost: KES 50,000–200,000 (~$380–1,500 USD) for a full assessment

## 5. ESTABLISHING A LOCAL MINING OPERATION {#5-local-mining-company}

### A. Company Registration

**Step 1: Register a Company**
- Register with the **Registrar of Companies** under the Companies Act, 2015 (Cap. 486)
- **Minimum requirement:** At least 1 director who is a Kenyan citizen
- **For artisanal/mining permits:** Must be 100% Kenyan-owned
- **For mining licences:** Must be at least 60% Kenyan-owned (Mining Act, s. 112)
- Cost: ~KES 10,000–15,000 (~$75–115 USD) for company registration
- Online portal: [eCitizen Business Registration](https://ecitizen.go.ke)

**Step 2: Register with Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA)**
- Get a PIN certificate for the company
- Register for VAT if annual turnover exceeds KES 5 million
- Cost: Free

**Step 3: Obtain County Business Permit**
- From Migori County Government
- Cost: ~KES 5,000–15,000 (~$38–115 USD) annually

### B. Mining License Types and Costs for Locals

#### Option 1: Artisanal Mining Permit (Best Starting Point)

**Mining Act, Sections 95-106**

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| **Who can apply** | Kenyan citizens only (individuals or cooperatives) |
| **Area allowed** | Up to 20 hectares |
| **Duration** | 2 years, renewable |
| **Cost** | Application fee: KES 1,000 (~$8 USD) |
| **Annual fee** | KES 1,000 (~$8 USD) |
| **Operations** | Traditional/customary methods; no heavy machinery |
| **Minerals** | All minerals except radioactive |
| **Where to apply** | County Director of Mines, Migori County |

**Documents Required:**
1. National ID or Passport (Kenyan citizens only)
2. PIN Certificate
3. Application Form (from Mines Office)
4. Sketch map of the area
5. Proof of land ownership or consent from landowner
6. Passport-size photographs

#### Option 2: Mining Permit (Small-Scale)

**Mining Act, Sections 85-94**

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| **Who can apply** | Kenyan citizens and companies (60%+ Kenyan ownership) |
| **Area allowed** | Up to 10 hectares |
| **Duration** | 5 years, renewable |
| **Cost** | Application fee: KES 10,000 (~$75 USD) |
| **Annual fee** | KES 20,000–50,000 (~$150–380 USD) depending on area |

#### Option 3: Mining Licence (Large-Scale — Future Goal)

**Mining Act, Sections 67-84**

| Requirement | Detail |

| **Who can apply** | Companies (60%+ Kenyan ownership) |

### C. Licensing Process Timeline

```
Week 1-2:    Register company, gather documents
Week 3-4:    Submit artisanal mining permit application to County Mines Office
Week 5-8:    Processing, inspection of site, public notice period
Week 9-12:   Permit issued (if approved)
TOTAL:       2-3 months for artisanal permit
```

### D. Financing with Mineral Rights as Collateral

**Challenge:** Most Kenyan banks are reluctant to lend against mineral rights because:
- Minerals are state property (not directly pledgeable)
- Mining is considered high-risk

**Options:**

1. **Kenya Development Corporation (KDC)** — Government development finance institution that supports mining ventures
2. **Women Enterprise Fund / Youth Enterprise Fund** — If family members qualify
3. **County Government Revolving Fund** — Migori County may have funds for local entrepreneurs
4. **SACCOs (Savings and Credit Co-operatives)** — More flexible than banks
5. **Equity Bank / KCB** — Have specific mining sector lending products
6. **Microfinance institutions** — Faulu, SMEP, etc. for smaller amounts

**Strategy:** Use the **artisanal mining permit + independent geological assessment** as evidence of potential income to secure financing.

## 6. CHALLENGING UNFAIR CONTRACTS {#6-challenging-contracts}

### A. Grounds for Nullification

If the family has already signed an agreement with Chinese miners, it may be challenged on these grounds:

**1. Lack of Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC)**
- Under **ILO Convention 169** and the **UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP)**
- Kenya is a signatory to both
- If the family wasn't given full information in a language they understand, consent was not "informed"

**2. Violation of the Mining Act**
- If the Chinese miners don't hold a valid mining licence or mineral dealer's licence
- If they didn't follow the compensation process under Section 172
- Mining Act, Section 163: It is **illegal** to deal in minerals without a licence
- Penalty: Fine up to KES 1,000,000 (~$7,500 USD) or imprisonment up to 5 years, or both

**3. Unconscionable Bargain**
- If the price paid was grossly below market value
- If there was a gross inequality of bargaining power
- Kenyan courts have recognized unconscionability as a ground for setting aside contracts

**4. Public Policy Violation**
- Under **Law of Contract Act, Section 23**: A contract is void if it is opposed to public policy
- Exploitative mineral contracts may fall under this

### B. Legal Procedure to Challenge

1. **Send a formal demand letter** (through a lawyer) to the Chinese miners
2. **File suit** in the **Environment and Land Court** (has jurisdiction over mining disputes)
3. **Seek interim injunction** to stop all mining operations pending the case
4. **Report to the Directorate of Mines** for administrative action

### C. How to Delay Chinese Miners Legally

**Immediate delay tactics (all legal):**

1. **Demand to see their mining licence** — If they can't produce it, they cannot legally mine. Report to police under Mining Act s. 163.
2. **Refuse entry to your land** — Under s. 172(2), miners cannot enter private land without compensation or a court order.
3. **File an objection** with the Mineral Rights Board against any licence application they've made.
4. **Request an EIA review** — Challenge their Environmental Impact Assessment with NEMA.
5. **File a court injunction** — Environment and Land Court can issue interim orders stopping operations.
6. **Report to County Assembly** — Your MCA (Member of County Assembly) can raise the issue in the county assembly.
7. **Petition the County Governor** — Under the County Governments Act, citizens can petition for action.

## 7. INTERNATIONAL FRAMEWORKS & BEST PRACTICES {#7-international-frameworks}

### A. African Mining Vision (AMV)

Adopted by the African Union in 2009, the AMV provides that:

- Mining must contribute to **sustainable development** and **poverty eradication**
- Communities must be **meaningful stakeholders** in mining governance
- **Transparent and accountable** management of mineral resources
- **Equitable sharing of benefits** between government, companies, and communities
- **Artisanal and small-scale mining** should be formalized and supported

**Kenya is bound by the AMV** as an AU member state. Use this in advocacy.

### B. Tanzania — Best Practice: Local Content Requirements

**Tanzania's Mining Act, 2010 (amended 2017):**
- Requires **local content** of at least 5% equity for Tanzanians
- Mandatory **mineral beneficiation** within Tanzania
- **State participation** of up to 16% in mining operations
- **Community development agreements** are mandatory
- **Fair compensation** for displaced communities

**Lesson for Kenya:** Tanzania has stronger local participation requirements. Advocate for similar provisions in Migori County.

### C. Botswana — Best Practice: Revenue Sharing

- Government holds **15% equity** in all mining operations
- **50/50 profit-sharing** with De Beers (diamond mining)
- Mining revenues fund **education and infrastructure**
- Strong **sovereign wealth fund** from mining revenues

**Lesson for Kenya:** Push for a Migori County mining revenue-sharing framework.

### D. Ghana — Best Practice: Small-Scale Mining Support

- **Minerals Commission** actively supports small-scale miners
- **Community mining schemes** where locals get mining leases
- **Mercury-free mining** technology support
- Dedicated **small-scale mining district offices**

**Lesson for Kenya:** Ghana's community mining scheme could be replicated in Nyatike.

### E. EITI (Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative)

Kenya is an **EITI candidate country**. This means:
- Mining companies must disclose payments to government
- Government must disclose mining revenues
- Communities can demand transparency
